Picks Campo Theory

How to win a prediction pool you can't out-forecast

This project picks scorelines for the 2026 World Cup. But its real subject is a subtler question than "who will win?" — it is how do you finish in the money in a pool of a million strangers? Those are not the same problem, and most of the interesting engineering lives in the gap between them. What follows is the theory, honestly: the parts that are textbook, the parts that are calibrated guesses, and the parts we still don't know.

1. The problem is not forecasting

A prediction pool — a prode, in Argentine Spanish — pays you for guessing match results. It is tempting to treat it as a forecasting contest: build the best model, maximize your expected points, done. That instinct is wrong, and seeing why is the whole point of this page.

The prizes don't go to the most accurate ticket. They go to the few tickets that finish at the top of a leaderboard. Concretely, this optimizer targets two very different pools that score the same 72 group-stage matches:

  • MP — the MercadoPago "Fixture 2026" public ranking, roughly one million entrants, with cash prizes only for the top three. Scoring: +6 for an exact scoreline, +3 for the correct outcome (win / draw / loss), 0 otherwise. This is winner-take-all.
  • EMP ("ELMASPRODE") — a small friends' pool that simply pays by points. Its scoring gives partial credit: 10 exact / 7 correct outcome plus one team's goal count right / 5 correct outcome only / 2 one team's goals right with the outcome wrong / 0 otherwise.

Here is the crux. In the friends' pool, your prize is your point total — so maximizing expected points is exactly the right objective. But in the million-entrant pool, your prize is a step function: it is enormous if you finish top three and zero otherwise. The quantity you actually want to maximize is

$$ \text{objective} \;=\; \mathbb{P}\big(\text{your ticket finishes top 3}\big), $$

which is not the same as \( \mathbb{E}[\text{points}] \). If you pick the same "obvious" scoreline as 600,000 other people and it hits, you are tied with 600,000 people and the tiebreakers decide — you have won nothing. To finish top three out of a million, you need your good outcomes to be outcomes that few others share. Finance has a name for this: you want right-tail exposure, even at the cost of a slightly lower mean. So the same match, scored under two different rulebooks, can demand two different picks. The optimizer computes both columns on every run.

2. Modelling a single match

Everything downstream rests on one object: for each match, a probability for every possible scoreline — a full \(6{\times}6\)-ish grid of \(\mathbb{P}(\text{A scores } i,\ \text{B scores } j)\). We build it in three steps.

2.1 From Elo to expected goals

We start from each team's Elo rating (sourced nightly from eloratings.net). The Elo difference between two teams maps to an expected score \(W_e\) — the share of a point team A would average against B, counting a draw as half:

$$ W_e \;=\; \frac{1}{1 + 10^{-\,\Delta r / 400}}, \qquad \Delta r = r_A - r_B \;(+\,100 \text{ if A is a host}). $$

The three host nations (Mexico, Canada, USA) get a +100 Elo bump in their own matches — a standard home-advantage proxy that makes the model's win probabilities line up with eloratings.net's published numbers. \(W_e\) is a single number, though, and we need two goal rates. We assume an expected total number of goals for the match and then solve for the split \((\lambda_A, \lambda_B)\) — the expected goals of each side — such that a Poisson model with those rates reproduces \(W_e\) exactly. (The solve is a one-dimensional bisection on the goal supremacy \(\lambda_A-\lambda_B\); nothing exotic.)

2.2 Poisson, and where it goes wrong

Goals in football are, to a first approximation, two independent Poisson processes. The probability a team with rate \(\lambda\) scores exactly \(k\) goals is

$$ \mathbb{P}(k \mid \lambda) \;=\; e^{-\lambda}\,\frac{\lambda^{\,k}}{k!}, $$

and the naive scoreline probability is the product \(\mathbb{P}(i\mid\lambda_A)\,\mathbb{P}(j\mid\lambda_B)\). This is clean and almost right. Its known failure is the low-scoring corner of the grid: real football has more 0-0, 1-1 and 1-0 results than two independent Poissons predict, because the two scores are not truly independent (a team protecting a lead stops attacking; a tight game stays tight).

2.3 The Dixon–Coles correction

Dixon and Coles (1997) patch exactly those four cells with a multiplicative factor \(\tau\) governed by a single correlation parameter \(\rho\):

$$ \tau_{\rho}(i,j) = \begin{cases} 1 - \lambda_A \lambda_B\, \rho & (i,j)=(0,0)\\[2pt] 1 + \lambda_A\, \rho & (i,j)=(0,1)\\[2pt] 1 + \lambda_B\, \rho & (i,j)=(1,0)\\[2pt] 1 - \rho & (i,j)=(1,1)\\[2pt] 1 & \text{otherwise} \end{cases} $$

The corrected, renormalized grid \(\mathbb{P}(i,j) \propto \tau_\rho(i,j)\,\mathbb{P}(i\mid\lambda_A)\,\mathbb{P}(j\mid\lambda_B)\) is the match model. So far, so textbook. The interesting part is the calibration.

2.4 A finding: the sign of \(\rho\) flips at the World Cup

In the club-football literature, \(\rho\) is reliably negative (around \(-0.13\)): pure Poisson under-predicts draws like 1-1, and a negative \(\rho\) pushes mass toward them. We started there. Then we fit \((\text{total goals},\,\rho)\) by maximum likelihood against the actual scorelines of 192 group-stage matches from the 2010, 2014, 2018 and 2022 World Cups — and the data asked for the opposite sign.

The best fit is an expected total of 2.80 goals and \(\rho = +0.05\) — small, but positive. The residuals say why: at the World Cup the model needs to move probability mass out of 1-1 and into 1-0, the reverse of the club-football pattern. The plausible story is structural: neutral venues and tactical caution in a one-month knockout tournament produce more genuine 1-0 grinds than the 1-1 draws that pad a 38-game domestic league. We trusted the 192 matches over the textbook constant. (Two honest caveats live here: with \(\rho\) this close to zero, turning Dixon–Coles off entirely costs almost nothing; and the best-fit total swings from 2.70 to 3.20 between tournaments — 2.80 is a robust prior, not a law. The residual under-prediction of 1-0 and 2-1 never fully goes away — it's a structural limit of forcing Poisson onto Elo, not something more parameter-tuning fixes.)

3. Borrowing the crowd's wisdom: betting markets

Where a liquid betting market exists for a match (Polymarket near kickoff, or a bookmaker's odds pasted in by hand), it usually knows more than our Elo model does, and we blend it in. But raw odds can't be read as probabilities directly, for a reason worth spelling out.

Bookmakers price the three outcomes so that the implied probabilities sum to more than 1 — typically 1.05 to 1.10. That excess is the vig (overround): the house's margin. To recover real probabilities you have to strip it out. The obvious method — divide each price by the sum so they total 1 — is subtly wrong. It spreads the margin proportionally, which systematically over-states longshots. This is the well-documented favourite–longshot bias: the public overbets longshots, so books pad longshot prices more than favourite prices.

We instead use Shin's (1993) method, which models the overround as arising from a fraction \(z\) of insider traders and solves for the true probabilities \(q_i\) under the bookmaker's break-even condition:

$$ q_i \;=\; \frac{\sqrt{\,z^{2} + 4(1-z)\,\pi_i^{2}/B\,}\;-\;z}{2(1-z)}, \qquad B = \textstyle\sum_i \pi_i, $$

where \(\pi_i\) are the offered (vig-inclusive) prices and \(z\) is found by bisection so the \(q_i\) sum to 1. The direction is the important bit: Shin raises the favourite's probability (by 1–3 points) and lowers longshots, correcting the bias rather than baking it in. When the offered prices already sum to 1, Shin is a no-op and reduces to plain normalization. The de-vigged market \(W_e\) is then blended with the Elo \(W_e\) at a chosen weight, and the whole scoreline grid is rebuilt from the blend.

4. Modelling the opponents you can't see

Recall the real objective: finish top three against a million other tickets. You cannot optimize against opponents you don't model — so we have to write down what the field will pick, without any data on the field. We do it with a mixture of behavioural archetypes, each contributing a per-match scoreline distribution:

  • Sharps (~15%): pick close to the true model, just slightly flatter — they roughly know what they're doing.
  • Casuals (~55%): take the favourite to win with a stock low-scoring line. Their mass piles onto a handful of scorelines — {1-0: 50%, 2-0: 25%, 2-1: 13%, 3-0: 7%, 3-1: 5%}. This concentration is the single most important fact about the field.
  • Patriots (~30%, only when the home country — Argentina by default — plays): pick narrative scorelines (a heroic 2-1, a 3-0) regardless of the odds.
  • Anti-rivals (~20% each, when Brazil or England play): a slice of the crowd that systematically bets against a rival, shrinking that team's win probability and shifting it to the draw and the opponent. (These are real social biases in the target audience, not decoration.)

Two subtleties make this model actually useful rather than a toy. First, the field is sampled player-level: each simulated entrant draws one persistent archetype and plays it across all 72 matches. A casual is a casual in every match — which means in a "chalk" tournament (favourites win, low scores) all the casuals cash together, fattening the right tail of the field's score distribution. That tail is precisely what decides the top three, and modelling it independently per match (the naive way) understates it by a wide margin. Second, beyond the parametric mixture there is an exact-field simulation that instantiates the real field at the million-plus scale — the latest runs use 4M tickets — and counts, universe by universe, how many beat ours: no extrapolation, just a very long overnight run.

5. The picker: why maximizing points loses the pool

Now we can state the core tension precisely. The points-maximizing pick — for a clear favourite, almost always the modal low score like 1-0 or 2-0 — is exactly the pick the casual majority also makes. Maximize expected points and you maximize your overlap with the crowd. When your pick hits, so does theirs, and you are tied with a third of the pool.

The fix is leverage: deliberately picking where the field is over-concentrated, buying scoreline variance while keeping the safer outcome floor. The picker first locks the modal outcome (preserving the 3-point floor for getting win/draw/loss right), then, among the scorelines consistent with that outcome, ranks each candidate \(s\) by a leverage utility:

$$ U(s) \;=\; \log \mathbb{P}(s \mid o^{*}) \;-\; \alpha \, \log \mathbb{P}_{\text{field}}(s \mid o^{*}) \;+\; \gamma \,\frac{i+j}{6}. $$

The first term wants the most likely scoreline. The middle term — with aggressiveness \(\alpha\) — penalizes scorelines the field crowds onto; it is the textbook winner-take-all contrarian term. The last term — with goleador bias \(\gamma\) — tilts toward goal-heavy lines, which both decorrelate from the 1-0/1-1 crowd and win two of the pool's tiebreakers (most exact scores, and most goals within exact scores). Set \(\alpha=\gamma=0\) and you recover plain points-maximization exactly.

How aggressive should the dials be? That is an empirical question, answered by Monte Carlo. We simulate thousands of whole-tournament "universes" (drawing a scoreline for every match from its grid), sample a realistic field for each, and score our candidate ticket against the field in every universe — the joint sampling matters, because our good days and the field's good days are correlated through the same scoreline draws. Then we extrapolate the rank-in-1000 statistic out to the real million-entrant tail (a Poisson approximation to a binomial), and finally confirm it with the exact-field simulation.

The headline result: a mild dial — \(\boldsymbol{\alpha = 0.25}\), \(\boldsymbol{\gamma = 0.40}\) — beats pure points-maximization by roughly 5× on \(\mathbb{P}(\text{top 3})\) against friends-sized and larger fields. The intuition that "more contrarian is better" has a hard ceiling, though: crank \(\alpha \ge 0.5\) and \(\mathbb{P}(\text{top 3})\) collapses — you start picking scorelines so improbable that they almost never come in, and a rare exact can't crown you if it doesn't happen. The sweet spot is "almost points-optimal, with a deliberate splash of variance." That tuning result — that strong contrarianism is a trap and the optimum sits just off EV-max — was the project's least obvious finding, and it only emerged once the field was modelled at realistic scale.

One last turn of the screw — and a correction. An earlier per-ticket Monte Carlo grid search, run against a small simulated field of a few hundred tickets, had picked something gentler still: \(\alpha = 0.00,\ \gamma = 0.20\), and that gentle dial was deployed for a while. But re-running the search as an exact field of 4M tickets, replicated across independent random seeds, exposed that gentle result as an artifact of the under-sized field. At realistic scale the conservative dial sinks to the bottom of the ranking — when millions share the chalk pick, the pick that buys no variance can't reach the top three — while \(\alpha = 0.25,\ \gamma = 0.40\) holds up robustly. So the deployed dial is now the research-grid optimum itself. The per-match picks are regenerated daily at that dial from the latest ratings and market; matches that have already kicked off keep the pick they carried into the match.

This match is a clean illustration of every piece at once. The grid is dominated by Mexico-wins-to-nil scorelines (the entire left column), because South Africa's expected goals are tiny. Points-maximization lands on 2-0, the single most probable line — and so does the friends'-pool pick, since with a favourite this heavy the partial-credit scheme agrees. The deployed MP dial (\(\alpha=0.25,\ \gamma=0.40\)), aimed at the million-entrant ranking, instead nudges to 3-0: almost as probable, goal-heavier, and held by ~12% of the simulated field versus the crowd piling onto 1-0 and 2-0. (As it happened, the match finished 2-0, so here the tilt took the outcome but missed the exact — 3-0 scores the +3 for the result, where 2-0 would have cashed the full +6. Over a whole tournament, any leverage tilt is a bet that being slightly off the crowd pays more often than it costs. One match never settles that bet — and this one went against it; the Monte Carlo over thousands of tournaments is what does.)

7. Two rulebooks, two tickets

Because the two pools score differently, the optimizer fills two columns on every match and marks where they diverge. The friends' pool (EMP) is pure points-maximization end to end — in a small pool paid by points, maximum expected points is the entire game, and any contrarian shift only throws points away. The public ranking (MP) gets the leverage treatment plus a knockout adjustment (in elimination matches the scoreline grid is convolved with a 30-minute extra-time grid for games level at 90'; penalty shootouts don't move the recorded marker).

How much do the two answers differ? It depends almost entirely on the leverage dial, not the scoring scheme: holding the picker fixed at plain points-maximization, the 6/3 and 10/7/5/2 schemes alone disagree on only a handful of matches — the partial-credit structure rarely overturns the modal line. At the deployed dial (\(\alpha=0.25,\ \gamma=0.40\)) the MP ticket parts ways with the EMP ticket in roughly 42 of 72 group matches — a concrete measure of how the aggressiveness knob, and it alone, trades accuracy for pool leverage. (The gentler \(\alpha=0,\ \gamma=0.20\) dial deployed earlier landed much closer to points-optimal, differing from EMP in just 4 of 72 — but, as the exact-field analysis showed, that left most of the available leverage on the table.)

8. What this model does not know

Honesty is part of the design. The hard limitations:

  • A tournament is one sample. Every \(\mathbb{P}(\text{top 3})\) here is an expectation over simulated universes. The real 2026 World Cup is a single draw from that distribution. A well-chosen ticket can still finish nowhere; that is variance, not a bug, and no model removes it.
  • The calibration is thin. 192 matches across four tournaments is a small sample, the per-edition goal regime swings widely (2.70 to 3.20), and the residual under-prediction of 1-0 and 2-1 is a structural limit of the Elo→Poisson mapping. The plan is to recalibrate the goal total once 2026's own first round provides data.
  • The field model is a hypothesis. Sharps, casuals, patriots and anti-rivals — with those exact weights — are an educated guess about a crowd we have no data on. The whole leverage conclusion rides on the crowd being as concentrated as we assume. We sweep the weights for robustness, but we cannot validate them against ground truth until the leaderboard is visible.
  • The knockout stage is unfinished. The extra-time convolution is in place, but the model's goal and correlation parameters are still the group-stage values; knockout football is a different, tighter regime that deserves its own calibration. The numbers here are most trustworthy for the 72 group matches.

None of these caveats undo the central idea, which is the part worth keeping: in a pool you cannot out-forecast, the winning move is not to predict better than everyone — it is to be wrong in a different direction than the crowd, and to size that bet exactly.
